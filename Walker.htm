Kelly Walker’s “Weeds”

Just pull your car up in front of her house.  I dare you.  Just go take a look at what the City [of Houston] now says is illegal.  You need to go even if you don’t like nature.  Even if you have never looked at a butterfly or a pupae.  You need to go especially if you think you know something about “weeds.”  Please. 

I pulled up alongside her front yard and stopped my car.  I was trying to read the posted signs about “nature preserve.”  A blue energy-efficient car pulled up, stopped, and then drove in the driveway.  I thought for a moment I should leave but her “weeds” had made it to the front page of the Houston Chronicle (June 27, 2005).  I guessed privacy wasn’t really the main issue at this point.

I saw someone in a flowered sleeveless top and khaki shorts bounce through the “weeds.”  I watched closely.  Was she old, eccentric, wizened, or crotchety? You know—all the things you might begin to imagine if the City was going to place a lien on her property.  The flowered top appeared to disappear in the “weeds” just before it lit right in front of me. She was smiling.  She taught me to see butterflies right there in front of me that I had missed for looking at the “weeds.”  The butterflies kept appearing out of nowhere.  “Wait!”  she said as she ran up to the front porch and came back with a jar.  Inside the jar was an unspectacular caterpillar slowly climbing up a plant.  “Have you ever seen one?” she asked.  Reaching out she fingered one leaf about the size of a dime amidst the millions of leaves on the “weeds” in her property.  The one she touched had a small hole in it.  “See,” she said. “I was hoping they would find this. Wait!”  she said again.  I was left standing in her front “weeds” trying to see caterpillars.  “Look,” she said. “Here’s one pupating.”  I looked and saw another glass jar with a plant and water.  This brown looking V-shaped thing was hanging down from the top of the jar.  “There,” she said.  I peered closer.  One-half caterpillar and one half pupae it looked, well, weird.  “Isn’t’ that cool?” she said and smiled again. “Wait!” she asked. Was she running back and forth or flitting?  She held a book in her hand.  “This is what it will look like later,” she said.    She pointed out patterns and colors of wings she was hoping to see and wings she had already seen this year.  She pointed below the pictures.  “See this?”  she said.  “It tells what each butterfly lives on.  I go out and get the seeds and spread them and then hope.”  She began pointing out the differences among the “weeds.”  See how similar they are?” she said.  “You could easily mistake one for the other.  Someone from some society came out and told me I needed to cut this weed down and this one,” she pointed and laughed.  “We each have our own thing,” she smiled. Her smile was infectious.  This was not an angry person despite $3000 worth of fines.  This was not a crazy person unable to make sense of the world or unable to care about the world.  This was a woman whose life was made up of knowing pupae, larvae, host plants, butterflies and “weeds.”  “Isn’t it cool?” she said.  “Thanks for coming by,” and she was gone again.

Within maybe the space of 15 minutes, the way I see in the world now, is well, weird.  Today I saw a hummingbird in mid-town while walking.  Now I don’t know what I might see next.  It’s very cool.  So, please.  If you think you know “weeds” and even if you know you don’t, please go by and spend a minute or two seeing.  Then call the City and let them know what you saw.       

Debbie Ebner @copyright 2005
