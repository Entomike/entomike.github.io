Citrus Root Weevil 

Diaprepes abbreviatus (Linnaeus, 1758)

First Houston-area Citrus Root Weevil 

Russ Pitman Park, Bellaire, Harris Co., Texas
July 27, 2009 (Don Verser)

Range: 

This weevil is native to the Caribbean. It was accidentally introduced and became established in Florida (1964), Rio Grande Valley (2000), Southern California (2005) and now apparently in Houston (July 2009).

Katy Emde found a second Citrus Root Weevil on July 30, 2009 at 6027 Glencove, Houston, TX. Google map showing both locations.

Adult Activity:

Although adults can emerge year round, their primary emergence period is May through October and November in central Florida.

Size: 3/8 to 3/4 inch (10 to 19 mm) long

Host Genera:

Acacia, Acer, Albizia, Ambrosia, Amyris, Baccharis, Bauhinia, Brassica, Caesalpinia, Cassia, Celtis, Citrus, Cordia, Crotalaria, Desmodium,
Diospyros, Erythrina, Ficus, Guaiacum, Hibiscus, Ilex, Indigofera, Jatropha, Juniperus, Magnolia, Manihot, Mimosa, Montezuma, Myrica,
Panicum, Passiflora, Persea, Phaseolus, Phoenix, Piper, Pithcellobium, Pittosporum, Prunus, Psidium, Quercus, Rhizophora, Rhus, Rosa, Roystonea, Rubus, Salix, Schinus, Schrankia, Senna, Sorghum, Ulmus, Zanthoxylum, as well as numerous crops - Texas Dept. Agriculture

Biology: 

Notching along the margins of young leaves is a telltale symptom of the presence of Citrus Root Weevil adults, or other related root weevils. 

The larvae of D. abbreviatus are found in the soil where they feed on the roots of the host. They will often girdle the taproot, impeding the ability of the plant to take up water and nutrients resulting in plant mortality. A single larva can kill young hosts while several larvae can cause serious decline of older, established hosts. Because larvae are below ground, it is difficult to detect them before decline of above ground potions of the host is observed. - Texas Dept. Agriculture

A single D. abbreviatus female can lay up to 5,000 eggs in her three to four month long life. - Univ. Florida
Quarantine: Texas Dept. Agriculture placed this species under quarantine in 2001 due to the population in south Texas.

Taxonomy: 

The Citrus Root Weevil is the only member of its genus in the U.S., however there are at least 16 additional species within the genus in the Caribbean.
